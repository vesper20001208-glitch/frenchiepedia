# French Bulldog Separation Anxiety

French Bulldogs are prone to separation anxiety. They are Velcro dogs that bond intensely with their owners and struggle when left alone.

## Why Frenchies Get Separation Anxiety

- Bred as companion dogs — being alone goes against their instincts
- Strong attachment to their primary person
- Sensitive and intuitive — they pick up on your emotions
- Lack of confidence and independence
- Traumatic experiences (rehoming, abandonment, owner loss)

## Signs of Separation Anxiety

### Mild
- Whining or barking when you prepare to leave
- Following you from room to room
- Excessive excitement when you return

### Moderate
- Destructive behavior (chewing doors, scratching walls)
- Urinating or defecating when alone (even if house-trained)
- Howling or barking when left alone

### Severe
- Self-harm (licking paws raw, tail biting)
- Refusing to eat or drink when alone
- Vomiting or diarrhea from stress
- Escaping attempts (breaking crates, digging through doors)

## Prevention (for Puppies)

1. Practice short departures (1-5 minutes) from day one
2. Do not make a big deal about leaving or returning
3. Crate train positively — the crate becomes their safe space
4. Leave interactive toys and stuffed Kongs
5. Gradually increase alone time

## Treatment (for Adult Dogs)

### Step 1: Vet Check
Rule out medical causes. Your vet may prescribe anti-anxiety medication for severe cases.

### Step 2: Create a Routine
Frenchies thrive on predictability. Consistent departure and return times reduce anxiety.

### Step 3: Desensitize Departure Cues
If picking up keys triggers anxiety, pick up keys and sit down. Repeat until it means nothing. Then jingle keys, walk to door, stop. Gradually build up.

### Step 4: Crate Training
A covered crate with a familiar blanket creates a den-like safe space.

### Step 5: Environmental Enrichment
Leave out: stuffed Kongs, puzzle toys, safe chews, a worn t-shirt with your scent.

### Step 6: Exercise Before Departure
A 15-minute walk before leaving tires them out. A tired Frenchie is less anxious.

### Step 7: Calming Aids
- Adaptil pheromone diffuser or collar
- ThunderShirt or calming wrap
- Calming music or white noise
- CBD treats (consult your vet first)

## What Not to Do

- Do not punish anxious behavior — it worsens the anxiety
- Do not make departures and returns emotional
- Do not get another dog as a solution — it rarely helps
- Do not use crates as punishment

## When to Get Help

Severe separation anxiety requires a certified veterinary behaviorist or a force-free trainer specializing in anxiety. Medication combined with behavior modification has the highest success rate.

With patience and training, most Frenchies learn to tolerate being alone. The key is consistency and never rushing the process.
